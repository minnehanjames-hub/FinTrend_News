§ 01 — Executive Summary

The Setup Heading Into This Week

Last week handed markets a one-two gut punch. U.S. payrolls dropped 92,000 in February — the third contraction in five months — and crude oil posted its largest single-week gain since futures trading began back in 1983. The cause isn't a mystery: the U.S.-Israel military campaign against Iran has effectively shut down tanker traffic through the Strait of Hormuz, the world's single most critical chokepoint for oil shipments. Futures markets spent the weekend pricing in what happens when that bottleneck holds. The answer: $100 oil at the open.

S&P 500 futures are already pointing lower this morning. Dow and S&P futures are off roughly 1.3–1.6%, while Nasdaq 100 futures are shedding closer to 2%. This isn't noise. This is a market trying to reprice a world where energy costs have surged more than 53% from the moment the conflict started — and nobody can tell you when it ends.

The week ahead isn't short on catalysts. Wednesday's February CPI print is the headline macro event. Friday brings Core PCE, the Fed's preferred inflation gauge. Sandwiched in between: Oracle earnings Tuesday, crude inventory data Wednesday, and whatever Trump posts on Truth Social about the war. Any one of those could move markets 1% in either direction. Buckle up.

§ 02 — Driver Analysis

What's Actually Moving This Market

Three forces are wrestling each other for control right now. None of them are subtle, and they don't all point the same direction.

🛢️
Oil Shock

WTI broke $90 Friday. Futures are testing $100 Monday morning. Qatar's energy minister floated $150 as a scenario if the conflict drags into weeks. A $20 sustained oil spike translates to roughly a 0.4% jump in headline inflation and a 0.1% drag on GDP — and that math gets uglier the longer Hormuz stays blocked.

📉
Payroll Shock

92,000 jobs lost in February. Consensus was expecting gains of at least 50,000. Revisions to December and January knocked off another 69,000. The Atlanta Fed's GDPNow model tumbled to 2.1% for Q1 — down nearly a third from 3.0% at the start of last week. The labor market is wobbling.

🔥
Stagflation Risk

Here's the nightmare scenario traders are pricing: rising energy costs reigniting inflation just as employment weakens. That traps the Fed. They can't cut without stoking inflation. They can't hike without burying an already-soft jobs market. San Francisco Fed President Mary Daly called it "a difficult policymaking environment" — that's Fed-speak for genuinely stuck.

🔒
Private Credit Stress

Not getting enough attention: BlackRock capped withdrawals on one of its private credit funds Friday after redemption requests hit 9.3% of shares — twice the repurchase cap. Blackstone's BCRED saw record redemptions earlier in the week too. Private credit stress tends to be a slow bleed, but when it surfaces it can accelerate fast.

§ 03 — Confirmation Signals

What the Data Is Confirming

The signals are mixed, but they lean defensive. February's S&P Global Manufacturing PMI held at 51.6 — expansion, but the slowest in seven months. New export orders fell for the eighth straight month. Retailers like Gap are already blaming winter storms for store closures. Meanwhile, the non-manufacturing PMI hit its highest reading in three-plus years at 56.1 — services still look sturdy.

The equity rotation tells you something too. Last week, money poured out of semiconductors (PHLX Semi Index down 4%, its worst week since November) and into energy and software. Marvell Technology surged 11% on strong AI-driven quarterly results — a reminder that the earnings story isn't dead, it's just getting selective. The stocks holding up best right now are cash-heavy, low-disruption names: think energy majors, defense contractors, and megacap tech with fortress balance sheets.

Treasuries saw notable selling all week — 10-year yields rose about 18 basis points — as oil's inflation implications spooked bond buyers. That's a headwind for rate-sensitive sectors like real estate and utilities.

§ 04 — Market Bias

This Week's Directional Read

Overall Posture
Cautious / Defensive

The risk/reward doesn't favor aggression right now. Oil at $100, job losses widening, and CPI due Wednesday with an upside surprise risk — that's not a cocktail for fresh long exposure. The VIX around 28 reflects elevated uncertainty, and until the Iran situation shows any sign of de-escalation, energy costs are a ceiling on consumer spending and a floor under inflation expectations. We're in "wait and confirm" mode.

§ 06 — Catalyst Watch

This Week's Event Calendar

  • MON 3/9 Market Open + Oil Watch. Futures already pricing in a rough open. Watch WTI — if it holds above $100, expect renewed pressure on consumer discretionary and transportation stocks. Any Trump comment on Iran ceasefire talks (or the lack thereof) will move things intraday.
  • TUE 3/10 Oracle Earnings (ORCL) + Existing Home Sales. Oracle reports after the close. AI cloud momentum is the story — investors want to know if enterprise tech spending is holding up despite macro headwinds. Home sales data will test housing resilience against elevated mortgage rates.
  • WED 3/11 February CPI — The Big One. Consensus expects headline CPI to tick up to 2.4% year-over-year from 2.3%. This data predates the oil shock, but any upside surprise reframes the Fed's entire calculus for 2026. Hot print = bonds sell off, rate cut expectations push further out. Crude inventory data also due — supply-demand signals from a Hormuz-constrained market.
  • THU 3/12 Adobe Earnings + Jobless Claims. Adobe is another AI bellwether. Weekly claims will be watched closely after the February payroll shock — any further deterioration in claims means the labor market is rolling over, not just wobbling.
  • FRI 3/13 Core PCE (January) — Fed's Preferred Gauge. This is the other inflation data point that matters. If PCE and CPI both show sticky or rising price pressure in the same week that oil breaches $100, the Fed's hands get tied. Kevin Warsh takes the chair in May. He'll inherit whatever mess this week creates.
§ 07 — Risk Scenarios

Bull Case vs. Bear Case This Week

Bull Case

De-escalation Spark

Iran signals any willingness to negotiate, even informally, and oil retreats from $100 fast. CPI comes in at or below consensus 2.4%, giving the Fed breathing room. Oracle beats on AI cloud revenue and lifts tech sentiment. Markets snap back 2–3% by week's end and the 6,880 level gets tested from below.

Bear Case

Stagflation Confirmation

Oil pushes toward $110 as Hormuz stays blocked. CPI surprises hot at 2.6%+. Jobless claims tick up, adding to the payroll weakness. The Fed goes on hold indefinitely. Markets re-test the 6,550 support zone and private credit stress headlines multiply. A genuine correction toward the 200-day moving average becomes the base case conversation.

§ 08 — Sector Positioning

Where to Look — and What to Avoid

Energy is the obvious winner in a $100 oil environment — upstream producers, pipeline operators, and LNG names all benefit directly. The sector was up nearly 9.4% in February before this week even started.

Defense has been the stealth play since the Iran strikes began. Lockheed Martin gained 6%, Northrop Grumman 5%, AeroVironment more than 10% in the first week of the conflict. With no near-term end to the war in sight, defense spending justifications only strengthen.

On the avoid side: Airlines, transportation, and consumer discretionary face margin compression every dollar oil climbs. Retailers are already dealing with storm-related disruptions and a softening consumer. Adding a fuel cost surge to that mix is not the setup you want to hold through.

Megacap tech — Nvidia, Microsoft, Apple — remains a relative safe haven. Cash-heavy, AI-driven earnings stories don't evaporate because of a Middle East conflict. But they're not immune either if the macro deterioration deepens.

§ 09 — Closing Note

From the Desk

Markets right now are living in the uncomfortable intersection of war, inflation, and a softening jobs picture. That's not a place where you want to be heroic. The playbook this week isn't about finding the next big winner — it's about not getting caught leaning the wrong way when Wednesday's CPI lands. Cash and patience are legitimate positions. The opportunities will come, but they'll come after clarity, not before. Stay grounded, stay flexible, and don't let FOMO talk you into a trade you haven't fully thought through. Watch oil. Watch CPI. The rest of the week will follow from those two data points.
