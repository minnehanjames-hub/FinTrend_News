Markets open this Monday morning carrying more unresolved tension than at any point since the Strait of Hormuz blockade began in February. The Iran peace framework — announced Sunday, expected to be formally signed by Thursday — sent oil tumbling in overnight trading and gave equities a bounce in futures. But the harder wall is directly ahead: on Tuesday and Wednesday, Kevin Warsh presides over his first Federal Open Market Committee meeting, and the stakes could scarcely be higher. Consumer prices are running at 4.2% year-over-year — a three-year high — and the man Trump appointed to eventually cut rates may instead be delivering the uncomfortable message that the next move is up.

The Week That Defines the Warsh Era

For months, Warsh's nomination was framed by markets as a signal of future rate cuts — a president who wanted lower borrowing costs appointing a chair who would deliver them. The world Warsh now inherits bears little resemblance to that projection. The Iran war's disruption of Strait of Hormuz shipping pushed headline CPI to 4.2% in May, the highest reading since April 2023. Core CPI came in at 2.9% — more contained but still well above the Fed's 2% target. Producer prices are running at 6.5% year-over-year. A hold on rates is near-certain — CME FedWatch puts the probability above 97%. But what markets are watching is everything else: the dot plot, the statement language, and how Warsh frames the path ahead in his first press conference as chair.

Will the dot plot shift from its March posture — which still penciled in a cut — to one that openly considers tightening? Market estimates have nine of eighteen officials penciling in at least one hike. Warsh is expected to announce he will not submit his own dot-plot projection, consistent with his long-held skepticism of forward guidance. Will he dismantle Powell's communication framework on day one? Each of these outcomes carries meaningful market risk, particularly for rate-sensitive equities and long-duration bonds.

"I don't believe in forward guidance. I think that the world is too uncertain for the Federal Reserve to be giving commitments about its future actions."

— Kevin Warsh, Senate confirmation hearing, 2026

Iran MOU: The Variable That Changes Everything

Sunday's peace framework is the most market-significant development since the Strait was closed in February. The 60-day ceasefire extension, with the Strait of Hormuz set to reopen, has already begun working its way through oil futures. If implemented fully, it would reverse the energy shock that drove headline inflation above 4% — handing Warsh the room he needs to hold and eventually pivot without ever having to hike. That is the bull case in one sentence.

The bear case is that a framework is not a signed agreement, and a signed agreement is not physical commerce. Gulf infrastructure has been damaged across four months of conflict. Major shipping lines have not yet confirmed resumption of Hormuz transits. Insurance rates remain elevated. Saudi Aramco's CEO warned in May that even if the Strait opened immediately, market normalization would take months, and potentially extend into 2027 if opening was delayed further. The market is pricing in a resolution that hasn't materialized in physical barrels yet.

SPCX: The Float Wall and the Second Week

SpaceX enters its second week as a public company with MSCI inclusion kicking in today — a structural tailwind from passive funds required to buy shares at a stock with only 4% of its cap in free float. The combination of low float and forced index-fund buying creates unusual supply-demand dynamics that could push the stock higher even as valuation-focused analysts flag it as dramatically overpriced. Morningstar's fair value sits at $62, against a close of $161 on Friday. The near-term catalyst calendar is thin — first earnings aren't due until August 6 — making technicals and sentiment the dominant drivers until then.

Monday Outlook

This week is a binary stress test for both peace optimism and monetary credibility. The base case — hold on rates, measured Warsh language, continued Iran progress — is constructive for equities. The tail risk — hawkish dot plot, stalled talks, SPCX volatility — is real and underpriced. The S&P's recent run has been remarkable. This week's data will determine whether the rally is earnings-driven durability or geopolitical-relief momentum. One of those has a longer half-life than the other.
