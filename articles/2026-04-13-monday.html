Situation Report
Twenty-One Hours. No Deal. A Blockade by Morning.

There was a moment late Saturday night in Islamabad when Pakistan's mediators thought they were close. American and Iranian negotiators had been in the same room for the first time since the 1979 revolution, grinding through 21 hours of talks at the Serena Hotel. Both sides said progress had been made on multiple issues. Then it all fell apart.

Vice President JD Vance emerged Sunday and delivered the verdict plainly: Iran "chose not to accept our terms." The sticking point, as it has always been, was nuclear. Washington demanded a full, verifiable end to uranium enrichment, dismantlement of enrichment facilities, and removal of over 400 kilograms of highly enriched uranium from Iranian soil. Tehran refused. Iran's Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi framed it differently — the two sides were "inches from an Islamabad MoU" when the U.S. moved the goalposts and announced the blockade simultaneously with negotiations still underway.

What happened next moved fast. Trump posted on Truth Social shortly after midnight: the U.S. Navy would begin blockading ships entering or exiting the Strait of Hormuz effective Monday at 10 a.m. ET. CENTCOM clarified the scope: the blockade applies to ships bound to or from Iranian ports, not vessels transiting to non-Iranian destinations. The U.S. will also interdict ships that have paid tolls to Iran — which Tehran had been charging at over $1 million per vessel — and mine-clearing operations are underway.

Status as of Monday Morning

The two-week ceasefire technically remains in force until April 22, but its practical status is now deeply uncertain. Iran's military spokesman warned that warships nearing the Strait would "be met with a strong and decisive response." The WSJ reports Trump is also weighing a resumption of limited military strikes. The UK declined to join the blockade but said it is working with France on a separate multinational freedom-of-navigation mission. Australia signaled readiness to participate if called upon.

Oil's reaction said everything. WTI surged more than 7% in early Monday trading, climbing back above $103 per barrel — nearly fully reversing last week's ceasefire-driven selloff. The brief window of relief that sent markets roaring on April 8 has closed. We are back, structurally, to the same supply disruption that has defined this crisis since late February.

Commodity Markets
The $100 Floor Is No Longer a Ceiling

When the ceasefire was announced on April 8, WTI fell 16.4% to $94.41 in a single session — its biggest one-day drop since April 2020. Brent shed 13.3% to settle at $94.75. The relief was enormous and, it turns out, premature. Even at those post-ceasefire levels, crude was still roughly $25–$30 above where it traded before the war began in late February.

The fundamental math hasn't changed. The Strait of Hormuz — through which roughly 20% of the world's oil and natural gas normally flows — remains effectively closed to commercial traffic. OPEC output dropped 7.9 million barrels per day in March due to the closure. Ras Laffan, the Qatari LNG complex that accounts for nearly 20% of global liquefied natural gas production, sustained damage that has reduced export capacity by an estimated 17%. That's infrastructure that won't recover overnight regardless of what happens diplomatically.

"If we have a blockade, we still have the problem of a shortage in the market of about 7 million barrels of crude, 4 million barrels of product not getting out. And we just added to that by making the Iranian barrels off the market."
— Karen Young, Senior Scholar, Columbia University Center on Global Energy Policy

Goldman Sachs, in revised forecasts issued following the ceasefire, trimmed its Q2 Brent target to $90 per barrel — but included the caveat that prices could average above $100 for the full year if the strait remains closed for another month. Given this morning's blockade announcement, that scenario is now the base case, not a tail risk. The Chicago Fed's Austan Goolsbee said Monday that if oil remained at $90/barrel month after month, it would "start spilling over to other prices" — a direct warning that the Fed's ability to stay on hold is being tested in real time.

Saudi Arabia has attempted to cushion the blow, restoring full pumping capacity through its East-West pipeline to the Red Sea and bringing the Manifa field back online. But alternate routes cannot fully substitute for Hormuz volume. The market is running a structural deficit, and the U.S. blockade — regardless of its stated purpose of reopening the strait — adds a new layer of uncertainty to tanker routing and insurance.

Earnings Season
Goldman Just Showed Us What Good Looks Like. Now Watch Everyone Else.

Here is the paradox this market is living with: the macro backdrop is genuinely ugly, and earnings are coming in strong. Goldman Sachs kicked off Q1 reporting this morning with results that would be impressive in any environment. Net revenues hit $17.23 billion, up 14% year-over-year. Diluted EPS came in at $17.55, versus $14.12 a year ago — a 24% increase. Return on equity of 19.8% was among the best in years. Assets under supervision reached a record $3.65 trillion.

The standout driver was Goldman's equities desk, which posted record revenues on the back of institutional portfolio churn as investors repositioned around the Middle East conflict and AI-driven sector rotations. Investment banking fees climbed 48% year-over-year, driven by an M&A revival that began late in 2025. FICC revenues did decline 10% — rates and mortgage intermediation was softer — but commodities and currencies desks more than offset that with war-driven volatility.

What Goldman's CEO Said on the Call

CEO David Solomon: "The combined effects of fiscal stimulus in developed economies, ongoing AI-related capital investment, and a more balanced regulatory agenda in the U.S. are powerful forces. At the same time, the geopolitical landscape remains very complex, and the ultimate impact of higher energy prices on inflation and growth is yet to be determined."

On the conflict's duration risk: "If the resolution drags, that probably will be a headwind in some of these areas, particularly inflation trends, as we get further into Q2 and Q3. At the moment, M&A and capital markets have been pretty resilient, and the environment continues to be quite constructive."

That cautious optimism will be tested by the week ahead. JPMorgan, Wells Fargo, Citigroup, Morgan Stanley, and Bank of America all report Tuesday through Thursday. Netflix, BlackRock, and Johnson & Johnson follow later in the week. The macro data calendar is equally loaded: March PPI drops Tuesday alongside JPMorgan earnings — that combination of producer inflation data and Dimon's commentary on consumer credit could set tone for the rest of the week. March CPI follows Wednesday. Empire State Manufacturing and NAHB Housing Market Index land Wednesday as well.

Investors will be listening for more than just headline beats. They want to know whether corporate America is actually starting to feel the oil shock in its cost structures, whether consumer credit quality is degrading, and — critically — whether managements are pulling forward guidance or simply saying "we'll see." Delta's CEO already flagged that the carrier will "meaningfully reduce" near-term capacity growth due to jet fuel costs. Expect that theme to echo across industrials and consumer discretionary reports in the weeks ahead.

The Macro Problem
Stagflation Is Not Hypothetical Anymore

The U.S. economy grew just 0.7% annualized in Q4 2025, well below the 2.5% initial forecast. U.S. employers shed 92,000 jobs in February — a number that was supposed to be a gain of 50,000. Consumer sentiment has been deteriorating since the war started pushing gasoline above $4. National gas prices rose more than 40% since the conflict began.

Against that backdrop, March CPI data landing Wednesday carries genuine weight. The conflict began on February 28, which means a full month of elevated energy prices will be baked into the March print. Energy's contribution alone could push headline CPI well above what the Fed had penciled in for Q1. The FOMC minutes drop the same day — those minutes cover the March 18 meeting, where Powell's tone was already shifting toward elevated uncertainty.

The Fed is boxed. Core inflation was already sticky before the war added an energy shock. Rate cuts that were expected in the first half of 2026 have been effectively pushed off the table. The market is pricing two cuts for the full year; private forecasters are now debating whether even one happens. Meanwhile, the economy is softening — not collapsing, but clearly losing momentum. That is the classic stagflation setup, and every week the Strait stays closed makes the arithmetic worse.

"For now and into the end of 2026, we are looking at elevated oil prices for certain. The question is not if they remain high — it's how high, and for how long."
— Karen Young, Columbia Center on Global Energy Policy, April 13

Editor's View
Where Things Actually Stand

The market spent last week trading a ceasefire that was never what it appeared. The April 8 relief rally was real — the Dow had its best day in a year, European indices posted their best sessions since 2022, VIX fell 22% — but it was priced on the assumption that the Strait of Hormuz would reopen, oil would normalize, and diplomacy would gradually resolve the conflict. None of that happened. Iran began charging $1 million tolls per ship. Only four vessels passed through on the day of the ceasefire itself. The backlog of 187-plus tankers holding 172 million barrels inside the Gulf remained essentially in place.

Now we are in a structurally different phase. The Islamabad talks represented the highest-level U.S.-Iran engagement since 2015 — and they failed. The U.S. response is not another deadline or threat; it is the literal encirclement of Iran's primary source of economic leverage. That is not a negotiating position that reverses quickly. Investors who bought the ceasefire rally and held through the weekend have spent Monday morning giving much of it back.

The one genuine bright spot is earnings. Goldman's numbers this morning were not just good — they were record-setting in equities, driven precisely by the volatility that the geopolitical crisis generates. JPMorgan, Morgan Stanley, and others will likely show the same dynamic. Market turbulence has been a direct revenue driver for trading desks. The question is whether that tailwind can offset the macro headwinds that are only beginning to show up in the data — and whether managements are still willing to provide constructive forward guidance in an environment that has moved from "complex" to "actively escalating" over a single weekend.

This week is heavy. Navigate it carefully.
