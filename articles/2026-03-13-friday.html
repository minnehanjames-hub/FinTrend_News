Three Weeks Down, No Floor in Sight

The S&P 500 closed Friday at 6,632 — its lowest print of 2026 — capping the index's third straight losing week. The Nasdaq shed another 0.93%, dragged down by tech names reassessing their valuation multiples against a world where oil is near $100 and rate cuts are vanishing from the calendar. The Dow, steadier but still red, lost 119 points on the day. None of the three indices have found a convincing bid since the strikes on Iran began in late February.

Oil wasn't done. WTI closed up 3.11% at $98.71 — Brent touched above $103 for the first time since August 2022 on Thursday and held most of those gains into the weekend. The new Supreme Leader's statement that the Strait of Hormuz should remain shut as a "tool to pressure the enemy" hit the tape mid-week and reset the floor on crude. Traders trying to fade the oil spike earlier in the week got reminded, again, that there is no off-ramp yet.

The GDP Revision Nobody Wanted

Friday morning front-loaded the pain. The Commerce Department revised Q4 2025 GDP down to a 0.7% annualized rate — well below the prior estimate of 1.4% and nowhere near the 1.5% consensus. The previous quarter had come in at 4.4%. That is a dramatic deceleration, and it wasn't caused by anything that's getting better. Weaker consumer spending in Q4 was the culprit, and Q1 hasn't exactly started with a consumer confidence surge — gas prices are meaningfully higher, and the University of Michigan sentiment index just hit its lowest reading of 2026.

At the same time, the February CPI print came in at 2.4% headline, 2.5% core — matching expectations, which would normally be a non-event. In this environment it means nothing. That data was collected before the oil shock took hold. Everyone knows the next few prints are going to be ugly, and bond traders have been pricing that in all week: the 10-year yield rose nearly 14 basis points on the week to 4.28%, the 30-year crept toward 4.90%. That is not the rate behavior of a market expecting rate cuts.

"Earnings are pretty good, but sentiment is difficult." The oil part of the sentiment and equity valuation embeds an interest rate path which is now being questioned. — David Aspell, CIO, Mount Lucas Management

February Jobs: The Number That Changed Everything

If GDP revision was the slow-burn, the NFP print that landed the week before was the blunt-force trauma. The economy shed 92,000 jobs in February — the first contraction since the pandemic era. The market had been conditioned to a slow-but-steady labor market. A negative print changes the Fed calculus instantly. Rate cuts should be the answer to a job contraction. But when oil is at $98 and threatening higher, you can't cut — you'd be throwing gasoline on inflation.

That is the bind. Slower growth and rising prices don't resolve themselves with a rate cut or a rate hike. They just coexist uncomfortably until something breaks or oil comes back down. The market spent most of this week figuring out that the Fed is functionally paralyzed, and it didn't like the conclusion. September was the last cut left on the futures calendar — and even that one is looking shaky.

The 70s Comparison Everyone Is Making

The OPEC embargo of 1973. The Iranian Revolution of 1979. Those are the historical anchors getting dragged into research notes this week, and they're not wrong to bring them up. Supply shocks that originate from geopolitical disruption rather than demand softening are the nastiest flavor of inflation — you can't solve them by tightening credit, you just wait them out and hope the economy survives. In 1973, the S&P 500 fell over 40% before it found a floor. That is the comparison nobody wants to say out loud but everyone is quietly stress-testing.

The 2026 vintage isn't identical. Core PCE at 3.1% isn't 1970s levels. The Fed funds rate at 3.5–3.75% isn't zero. But the structure is uncomfortably similar: an oil shock layered on top of an already-slowing economy, with a central bank that has limited room to respond. The VIX closed above 27 on Friday. It was below 20 two weeks ago.

Winners, Losers, and the AI Trade Stalling

Friday had its individual stories. Adobe tumbled more than 5% — CEO Shantanu Narayen, who had been in the role since 2007, announced he would step down once a successor is named. That overshadowed a solid earnings beat. Ulta Beauty cratered 12% after a Q4 miss on earnings per share. Both names reflect a market with zero patience for any disappointment right now.

On the positive side, Exxon Mobil hit all-time highs as energy continues to be the only sector getting a clean bid. The rotation from growth to energy that started the moment the Hormuz disruption became real has not reversed. If oil stays elevated, the math for energy majors is extraordinary — and it's doing nothing good for the technology and consumer names that carried the market through 2024 and most of 2025. Nvidia faces Kharg Island uncertainty the same way everyone else does: every dollar of AI capex budget gets harder to justify when energy and input costs are rising.

What's Next: FOMC Week

The coming week is the biggest macro event of the first half: the FOMC meets Tuesday and Wednesday, with the rate decision and updated dot plot on Wednesday at 2 p.m. ET. Nobody expects a move on rates, but that's almost beside the point. What the market wants is some signal from Powell about how the Fed is thinking about the oil shock, the negative NFP, and the GDP revision together. If he threads that needle poorly — too hawkish on inflation, too dismissive of growth risk — the S&P is looking for support around the 200-day moving average it hasn't tested since early 2025. If he's reassuring, there's a case for a bounce. But relief rallies in a war-driven market tend to be short.

PPI also hits Wednesday morning. Two hot PPI prints in a row going into an FOMC decision is not a comfortable setup. Micron and FedEx report earnings this week as well — Micron in particular matters as a read on tech demand against a backdrop of rising costs. Keep your seatbelt on.
