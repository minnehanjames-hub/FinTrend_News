The AI Trade Gets
a Stress Test

SPCX shed 16% Monday — its worst single session — as megacap tech sold off hard and South Korea's chip market cratered overnight. Micron reports Wednesday after years of blowout quarters. Core PCE lands Thursday. The week will tell us whether the AI rally has fundamental footing or is sitting on froth.

By Hunter William Lang & James Thomas Minnehan
MON 22 JUN 2026  ·  06:00 ET

Markets came back from the Juneteenth long weekend carrying the weight of last week's Fed shock — and immediately compounded it. Monday saw SPCX plunge 16.4%, its second-largest single-day loss on record after Nvidia's ~$590 billion drop last year. Google, Amazon, Meta, and Microsoft dragged the Nasdaq lower. South Korea's KOSPI fell 9.99% — its steepest drop in three months — as overseas investors dumped chip stocks after regulatory signals suggested the sector's rally had become overheated. Micron shed 10.25%, Qualcomm −8.59%, Arm −8.41%. SpaceX has now shed more than $600 billion in market value in three consecutive sessions, falling from $225.64 to below its first-day opening price of $150. This week's question is simple: is this a healthy rotation out of crowded positioning, or the beginning of a more structural repricing of the AI trade?

What Actually Happened Monday

The sell-off was concentrated but not universal — a critical distinction. While megacap tech and semiconductors cratered, consumer staples rose 1.7% to lead all sectors. Advancing shares led declining ones on the NYSE in high-volume trading. Market breadth at the broader S&P level actually improved, with the percentage of stocks above their 50-day moving average rising to 58% from near 50% — a potential signal of rotation out of the most crowded mega-cap names into a broader collection of stocks rather than an outright market breakdown. The 50-day moving average of the SPX at 7,339 and the 61.8% Fibonacci level at 7,120 represent the key technical support levels to watch if the sell-off extends.

SpaceX added to the risk-off mood with a two-pronged announcement: a $20–25 billion senior unsecured bond offering that reportedly met skeptical buyers in the debt market, requiring a premium to clear, and continued fallout from last week's Cursor acquisition. Bloomberg characterized the bond market's reception as cool — a signal that institutional fixed-income investors see SpaceX's valuation as stretched even if equity investors have been more forgiving.

Micron Wednesday: The AI Demand Proxy

Wednesday's Micron earnings after the close are the most important single report of the week — and arguably of the month. The stock is up more than 300% year-to-date entering earnings, making investor expectations extremely elevated. Analysts expect earnings of $20.83 per share on revenue of $35.75 billion. Last quarter, Micron delivered blowout results and guidance, yet the post-earnings reaction was bearish — a signal of how high the bar sits. The read-through is not just about Micron: it is the AI infrastructure demand signal that the entire semiconductor trade is anchored to. A beat-and-raise confirms the AI buildout is accelerating. A miss or cautious guide calls into question whether the 300%+ runs across chip names are earnings-justified or multiple-expansion stories that are now vulnerable to re-rating.

The PCE Variable That Decides September

Thursday's May core PCE is the data point that will move the September hike probability more than anything else this week. The Cleveland Fed's nowcast model projects core PCE at 3.3% — unchanged from April. If it prints at 3.0% or below, the disinflationary narrative gets a meaningful boost and September hike odds fall sharply. If it comes in at 3.4% or above, the committee's hawkish dots from last week gain credibility and the market will need to price a September move with much higher probability. Mohamed El-Erian flagged the current market setup as unusual: rising bond yields and falling oil prices simultaneously, driven by "expectations for a more hawkish Fed, inflation concerns, and the prospect of large and growing corporate and government bond issuance." That combination is not easily resolved in a single week.

Monday Outlook

Monday's tech selloff looks like froth clearing out of crowded positioning rather than fundamental deterioration — but the distinction matters less if the selling accelerates. The AI trade's resilience over the next 72 hours depends on two data points: Micron's results Wednesday night and core PCE Thursday morning. A Micron beat-and-raise followed by a soft PCE print would stabilize markets and re-anchor the bull case. The inverse — a cautious Micron guide and a hot PCE — would validate both the hawkish dot plot and the sector rotation in a way that is difficult to reverse quickly. Watch breadth: if the sell-off remains concentrated in mega-cap names while the broader market holds, this is a healthy reset. If it spreads into small-caps and defensives, it is something else entirely.
